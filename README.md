Its digital coverage is comprehensive—spanning all academic, administrative, medical, research, and public-facing systems. UW provides strong technical tools including Ally for Canvas, DubBot for website scanning, and extensive audio/video accessibility guidance. Developer and content-creator support includes detailed checklists, archived content rules, social media accessibility guidance, and a Course Materials Checklist for AY 25–26. Multi-phase compliance workflows (Discover → Initiate → Build → Refine → Transform) and coordinated project teams for 2024–26 show advanced monitoring and technical preparation.                                                                                                                                                    |UW organizational readiness is driven by one of the strongest governance structures among all evaluated institutions. Accessibility is positioned as a campuswide responsibility. Governance includes the ADA Digital Accessibility Initiative, ADA Digital Accessibility Governance Board, Enterprise Risk Management (ERM) alignment, Information and Technology Governance (ITG), and sponsorship from UW-IT, the Provost’s Office, Compliance Services, UW Medicine, and the Civil Rights Compliance Office. Responsibilities are clearly distributed across academic units, content creators, IT groups, project management teams, procurement units, and social media creators. Ongoing training (Digital Accessibility Basics, Ally, DubBot), support desks, and course-level guidance further reinforce institutional coordination.                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                              |UW environmental readiness, strongly aligned with DOJ Title II requirements, WCAG 2.1 AA, and the April 24, 2026 mandate. The university provides precise interpretations of legal exceptions, matching DOJ language exactly. Stakeholder engagement is deep and cross-institutional, involving faculty, staff, IT, developers, instructional designers, medical systems, research units, and compliance offices. UW uses enterprise-level external tools (Ally, DubBot) and publishes regular accessibility news and guidance. Transparency is strong, with publicly available timelines, initiative overviews, support resources, and compliance expectations. |Purdue University (Purdue)                      |Purdue demonstrates technological readiness, with explicit adoption of WCAG 2.1 AA and full alignment with the DOJ April 24, 2026 Title II digital accessibility rule. The scope of coverage is exceptionally clear: all websites, applications, mobile apps, instructional materials, and third-party technologies must be accessible—academic course content and vendor content are no longer exceptions. Purdue supports accessibility through enterprise tools such as Siteimprove, the Assistive Technology Lab (ATL), Microsoft 365 accessibility checkers, Brightspace LMS guidance, and the Libraries’ Digital Accessibility Skills guide. Instructor-facing technical support includes the Digital Instructional Materials Accessibility Checklist, captioning expectations, and document/media remediation guidance, while a multi-campus change initiative drives structured workflows, monitoring, and systemwide implementation.     |Purdue’s organizational readiness is grounded in strong institutional commitments and governance. The Electronic Information, Communication & Technology Standard (S-5) formalizes digital accessibility expectations and is enforced by the Office for Civil Rights (OCR). Governance also includes a systemwide project team across all Purdue campuses and the Innovative Learning team (Center for Instructional Excellence, Libraries & School of Information Studies, Purdue Online, and Teaching & Learning Technologies). Responsibilities are clearly defined for instructors (who must make all course materials accessible), content creators, departmental units, vendor managers, the ATL (assistive technology support), and the Disability Resource Center (student-facing access). Capacity-building is robust, including workshops, daily drop-ins, asynchronous Brightspace training, curated guides, and repeated campuswide communications preparing instructors for Title II compliance.                                                                                                            |Purdue demonstrates environmental readiness, directly citing the DOJ’s revised ADA Title II rule, the April 24, 2026 compliance deadline, and required WCAG 2.1 AA conformance. Messaging clearly communicates that accessibility is a shared responsibility and a civil rights requirement grounded in Purdue’s mission of learning, discovery, and engagement. Stakeholder engagement is broad, spanning all campuses and targeting instructors, staff, and vendors through multi-channel communication, workshops, fact sheets, and guidance pages. Purdue leverages external tools (Siteimprove, Microsoft 365 accessibility, DOJ resources) and supports assistive technology adoption through ATL and the DRC. Public transparency is strong, with published pathways for reporting inaccessible content and clear explanations of Purdue’s obligations, priorities, and preparation activities.|

Messaging consistently states “accessibility is everyone’s responsibility,” supported by required courses, webinars, and recurring capacity-building efforts across faculty, staff, and communicators. Responsibilities are distributed across content creators rather than centralized in IT, with OIT and the CAES Web Team providing support, training, and oversight.                                                                                                                                                                                                                   |UGA shows high environmental readiness, with explicit alignment to the April 24, 2024 DOJ Title II rule, the April 24, 2026 compliance deadline, and WCAG 2.1 AA standards. Transparency is strong: the Digital Accessibility Handbook is public, expectations are clearly communicated, and support channels (e.g., caesweb@uga.edu) are openly provided.
